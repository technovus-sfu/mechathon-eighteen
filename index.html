  • FAQ

    Who can participate?

    Great solutions come when many people with different backgrounds put their minds together. We’re accepting students from all backgrounds as long as they are over the age of 18. There will also be mentors and workshops in both the health and tech industry to give you all an even playing field.

    How big can my team be?

    Teams need to be composed of 3 to 4 people.

    What if I don’t have a team yet?

    Don’t worry, there are many people that sign up by themselves for Mechathon. On the day of the event, we will run an activity that will help you meet new people and form a team.

    What if I don’t have programming or engineering experience?

    Mechathon is an inclusive community that strives to help bring in interdisciplinary teams. It is your team’s passion, strategy, and presentation that will matter the most. In the past, some of our participants were able to get their ideas across with hand-made drawings. Additionally, we will have both technical and non-technical awards and a basic beginner robotics crash course for non-technical people.

    What should I bring?

    We recommend you to bring whatever you think you need so that you can brainstorm, design, and build. Bring a laptop and something to take notes with. We will have some basic hardware available for you such as cardboard, micro-controllers, sensors, LED’s , and soldering stations. A complete list of the hardware we will have available will be released closer to the event. Also, wear something comfortable so that you can focus all of your efforts on innovating and problem solving!

    How much does it cost?

    It’s free! Food and swag is included with your registration.

    What should I bring?

    Please bring ID that has your picture and name on it for registration. We recommend you to bring whatever you think you need so that you can brainstorm, design, and build. Bring a laptop and something to take notes with. We will have some basic hardware available for you such as cardboard, micro-controllers, sensors, LED’s , and soldering stations. A complete list of the hardware we will have available will be released closer to the event.

    I just graduated, can I still come to the event?

    Yes, we are accepting graduates. Please do come!

    Can I be reimbursed for travel expenses if I don’t live in the area?

    Unfortunately, we are not able to give any travel reimbursements for this year’s event. However, we might have it next year!

    What if I can only come for part of the event?

    As long as people in your team are able to attend the opening and pitch ceremonies, you don’t need to be there for the whole event.

    What hardware will be provided?

    We will have basic robotics tools such as arduino unos, wires, sensors, LED’s, cardboard, etc for you to borrow. A more detailed list of our hardware will be released closer to the event date.

    Where can I find more answers?

    For more information, please email John at Technovus.sfu@gmail.com

    What happens when I win?

    The first place team will get the chance to implement their solution in a self-directed project with the mentorship of Dr. Carolyn Sparrey who has extensive experience in creating health care technologies. We will also have prizes for the runner ups. If you’re someone who has no technical experience, we also have prizes for non-technical solutions.

    JUDGES AND MENTORS

    Julielynn Wong

    Founder Chairman & CEO of 3D4DMD

    Julielynn is an award-winning, Harvard educated physician-scientist, innovator, and educator whose life’s mission is to use technology to benefit the world. She is internationally recognized as a 3D printing, drone, robotics, telemedicine and digital health pioneer who uses cutting-edge technology to deliver healthcare solutions in diverse environments, from outer space to remote communities with limited access to healthcare resources. She was the first to 3D print medical supplies onboard the International Space Station and at the Mars Desert Research Station. She designed a solar-powered mobile 3D printing system that can be transported in a carry-on suitcase to produce medical supplies in remote, off-grid communities. Julielynn regularly lectures on innovation in healthcare and is a frequent contributor to various media outlets including ABC News, Forbes, and the Huffington Post.

    Carolyn Sparrey

    Principal Investigator PCHT, Associate Professor SFU

    Carolyn Sparrey, PhD, is the lead Principle Investigator (PI) of Point of Care Health Technologies and the Rapidtech co-lead. She is an associate Director and an Executive Committee member of the STAR (Science and Technology for Aging Research) Institute. Dr. Sparrey is an Associate Professor in the School of Mechatronics Systems Engineering at Simon Fraser University. She obtained her B.A.Sc. from the University of Waterloo and her M.A.Sc. from the University of British Columbia. Dr. Sparrey completed her Ph.D. in mechanical engineering at the University of California, Berkeley, and a Post-Doctoral Fellowship in Neurosurgery at the University of California, San Francisco.
    Dr. Sparrey’s research focus is on biomechanics and biomaterials. She also works on modeling and simulating injuries and diseases, including accident reconstruction. Dr. Sparrey also designs and evaluates injury prevention devices. Dr. Sparrey leads Simon Fraser University's Neurospine Lab, working closely with surgeons and biologists at ICORD and the California Spinal Cord Injury Consortium to develop injury models, identify injury prevention strategies and design algorithms to improve injury diagnostics.

    Peter Payne

    Entrepreneur In Residence BCIC

    Peter has founded/co-founded eight companies in diverse sectors, from electronic publishing to medical devices. Those companies sold products in Canada, USA, Europe and Asia. He has been involved with government organizations, private and public companies, reverse take-overs, venture capital corporations and not-for-profit groups. He is past Director of Programs at the BC Technology Industry Association, and Executive-in- Residence for health technologies. Peter earned a PhD in Pathology (UBC) and was a Clinical Instructor and Honorary Lecturer at UBC’s Faculty of Medicine.
    He was a member of the UBC Faculty of Medicine and is currently on Faculty with Simon Fraser University’s Beedie School of Business. Peter is also currently an Entrepreneur in Resident at BCIC. Peter has been an invited speaker at international conferences, holds a number of patents and has received several awards for academic and business activities. Peter often speaks about the application of the scientific method to innovation challenges.

    Veronica De Jong

    Research Coordinator, Saint Paul’s University

    Veronica de Jong is the Research Coordinator at Community Health Research Team (CoHeaRT) based in St. Paul's Hospital. Veronica completed her MSc in McGill and has been coordinating impactful research at St. Paul's Hospital for than five years. Veronica went back to McGill as a guest lecturer and has a tremendous amount of experience working with various research institutions working in clinical research and healthcare.
